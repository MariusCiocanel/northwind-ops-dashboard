# How we write the morning briefing

This is the house style for the daily briefing Dana, Marcus, and Priya read each morning. When you build the briefing (and the skill that generates it), follow this — the point is that they can glance at it over coffee and immediately know what changed and what to do.

## Principles

- **One screen, 60 seconds.** If they have to scroll twice, it's too long.
- **Lead with the decision, not the data.** Start with what changed and what needs attention, then show the numbers that back it up.
- **Plain language.** No jargon, no "synergy," no hedging. Write like a sharp colleague, not a dashboard.
- **Numbers always carry context.** Never "$4,120." Always "$4,120, down 8% from last week."
- **Tie back to the priority metrics.** Everything should ladder up to what the team flagged in the leadership sync. If it's not on that list, it probably doesn't belong in the briefing.
- **Be honest about bad news.** If a roast is sliding or a SKU is about to stock out, say so plainly and first.

## Structure

1. **Headline** — one or two sentences: the single most important thing about the week.
2. **Watch list** — 3–5 bullets, each a metric that moved or a risk that's building, with the number and its trend.
3. **Recommended actions** — a short, concrete to-do list. Each item names the SKU/area and the action ("Reorder Ethiopia — 0.6 weeks of cover, PO already in transit, confirm the date").

## Tone examples

- Good: "Ethiopia is stocked out again — 0.6 weeks of cover. The replenishment PO lands ~June 9; until then we're leaving money on the table on our fastest grower."
- Too vague: "Inventory levels for some products are running low and may need attention."
- Too much: a paragraph explaining what weeks-of-cover is. They know. Just give them the number and the call.

Keep it tight, keep it honest, keep it pointed at a decision.
